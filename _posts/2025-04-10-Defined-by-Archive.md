# I. The Fragility
{: .didot }

*What we call the self is but a scattering of moments, most of which dissolve quietly across time.*
{: .cmu-serif }

Memory, for all its warmth, deceives. It smooths the edges, reorders narratives, cloaks regret in sentiment. The mind hardly retrieves experience as it was; it reassembles from fragments, softening with our yearning, and distorting with our fears.

Psychologists call it reconstructive memory - Each act of remembering is a kind of creation, but also a quiet forgetting. We do not recall the past as it truly was, but as it suits the present self. *Tempora mutantur, nos et mutamur in illis* - times change, and we change with them.

Thusly, if the self is defined solely by memory, it will forever be in flux. How then, do we anchor ourselves drifting on the seas of time?

<p class="notice--info" style="background-color: #00214724; border: 1px solid #00214705; max-width: 70%;"><span class="barlow" style="color: #f8e6b1d2"><i>Tempus edax rerum
</i></span>: Time, devourer of all things.</p>


# II. The Anchor
{: .didot }

One interesting thing about the past - it brushes against us unexpectedly. A faint trace of a perfume that summons someone bygone, a song that brings back an unreturning afterglow, these sensations act as anchors that bind us closer to our former selves. But what if we could hold onto something more tangible, something that resists the erosion of time? Namely, <i class="cmu-serif">the archive</i>?

By <i class="cmu-serif">the archive</i>, I do not mean a systematic collection since childhood, but something far smaller and more intimate. They are the physical traces that bear witness to a life. Letters, postcards, diary pages, marginalia in books - all the debris of having once been somewhere, with someone, as a particular self.

Unlike memory, ink does not bend to our present needs, and paper yellowed by time does not politely forget. <i class="cmu-serif">the archive</i> is thus not an extension of memory, but its counterpoint: where memory is fluid, <i class="cmu-serif">the archive</i> persists. They are the fragments that we have endured, a testament, a proof of existence.

<p class="notice--info" style="background-color: #00214724; border: 1px solid #00214705; max-width: 70%;"><span class="barlow" style="color: #f8e6b1d2"><i>Verba volant, scripta manent</i></span>: Spoken words fly away, written ones remain.</p>

Psychologically, this is crucial. A certain thought was once put to paper, a certain feeling once took the risk of being recorded. In a world of impermanence and informational overload, we are threatened by amnesia, cultural and personal. <i class="cmu-serif">The archives</i> offer a stubborn point of reference, as they quietly insist that at some point, something was there.

# III. The Inconsistency
{: .didot }

The archive becomes a site of estrangement - a place where you confront the fact that you’ve changed, but the world still remembers otherwise. This mirrors Derrida’s idea that the archive *always works, and a priori, against itself*, because it both preserves and alienates. It freezes what was, while the self has moved on.

Thus, the contradiction is not a flaw but a philosophical necessity. You are both archivist and stranger to your own past.

The right attitude is neither romanticisation nor rejection—it is witnessing.
To hold an archive is not to relive the past or to demand it still be true. It is to acknowledge its former truth—and in doing so, to honour the reality of who you once were. That self may be gone, but the trace remains. And sometimes, that trace is all that’s needed to remember that something was real.
The archive does not ask you to return. It only asks that you do not deny. There is wisdom in amplius videre—to “see more deeply,” to look at the past with both tenderness and distance.
To reconcile with this tension is to accept that truth need not be eternal to be meaningful. A fragment from the past can remain true to then, even if it no longer defines now.

Archiving is an act of preservation without possession. You keep a letter not to relive the relationship, but to acknowledge that it once existed. You revisit a diary entry not to return to that mindset, but to witness how far you’ve come—or how fragile we all are.
To drown in the past is to refuse the present. To archive it is to give the past a resting place, so that it does not haunt your becoming.
